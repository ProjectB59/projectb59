B59-0096 · WRITING · PROOF-OF-WORK

Pricing via Processing, or Combatting Junk Mail

Cynthia Dwork & Moni Naor · CRYPTO ’92 · August 1992
“In order to send a message, the sender must compute some moderately expensive, but not intractable, function of the message and some additional information.”

Why it matters

This is the paper that invented proof-of-work. Long before anyone spoke of mining, Cynthia Dwork and Moni Naor asked a narrow, practical question: how do you stop junk mail and the abuse of a shared resource without a central gatekeeper or a payment system?

Their answer was economic, not bureaucratic. Make the sender do a small amount of computational work — solve a “pricing function” — before a message is accepted. For a person sending a handful of messages the cost is invisible. For a spammer sending millions, the cost becomes prohibitive. Access is rationed by CPU cycles instead of identity, permission, or money.

The mechanism

Dwork and Naor specified pricing functions that are moderately hard to compute but easy to verify, and that can be tied to the specific message and recipient so the work can’t be pre-computed or reused. They even described a shortcut — a trapdoor the authority could use — so that the same scheme could implement more general access control.

That asymmetry — hard to produce, trivial to check, bound to its inputs — is the exact property every later system relies on.

The lineage it started

Hashcash (Adam Back, 1997) turned the idea into a deployable email stamp. b-money (Wei Dai, 1998) and bit gold (Nick Szabo) used proof-of-work to create money. RPOW (Hal Finney, 2004) made the tokens reusable. And in 2008 Bitcoin made proof-of-work the heartbeat of a decentralized ledger. The Bitcoin whitepaper cites Dwork and Naor directly.

Every time a miner hashes a block, it is running the descendant of an idea first written down to fight spam.

Original abstract (excerpt)

We present a computational technique for combatting junk mail in particular and controlling access to a shared resource in general. The main idea is to require a user to compute a moderately hard, but not intractable, function in order to gain access to the resource, thus preventing frivolous use.
